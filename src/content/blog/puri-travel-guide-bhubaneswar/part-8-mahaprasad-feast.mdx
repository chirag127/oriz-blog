# Part 8: The Divine Feast — Mahaprasad at Ananda Bazar

No pilgrimage to Puri is complete without partaking in the Mahaprasad. The food offered to Lord Jagannath is considered so sacred that it transcends all caste and religious boundaries; anyone can share it from the same pot. 

Located in the northeast corner of the outer enclosure of the temple is **Ananda Bazar** (The Market of Joy). It is reputed to be the largest open-air food court in the world, where food cooked by hundreds of *Suaras* (temple cooks) in the massive temple kitchens is sold daily.

## Is Mahaprasad Free?

You asked: *"Is Mahaprasad free? Is Abada free or not? What is free and what is not free?"*

**The short answer is: No, Mahaprasad (Abhada) is not free.**

*   **What IS Free:** Sometimes, priests may hand you small pinches of dried, cooked rice (called *Nirmalya* or *Kaibalya*) as a blessing. The darshan (Sahana Mela) is free. 
*   **What is NOT Free:** The full, cooked meal—consisting of rice, dal, besara (mixed veg with mustard), saga (spinach), and sweet dishes—must be purchased. The temple kitchen operates on a massive scale without direct government subsidies for the food itself, so the cooks sell the prasad to sustain the ecosystem.

## Costs and Types of Mahaprasad

There is no officially enforced, fixed price menu hanging on a digital board. Prices vary based on the size of the earthen pot (*Kudua*), the number of items, and sometimes the day's demand.

*   **Typical Cost for Two:** For you and your mother, a satisfying meal consisting of Anna (rice), Dal, Besara, and Mahura (a vegetable curry) will typically cost between **₹150 to ₹300 total**.
*   **Sweets (Pitha and Khaja):** Dry sweets like the famous Puri Khaja or various Pithas cost extra, usually ranging from ₹50 to ₹100 per packet depending on size.
*   **The Best Time to Eat:** The freshest *Abhada* (the midday offering) arrives at Ananda Bazar between **1:30 PM and 2:30 PM**. By arriving at the temple around 10:00 AM, finishing darshan by 12:30 PM, you will be perfectly timed to buy the freshest, hottest Mahaprasad.

## How to Buy for the Best Experience

Navigating Ananda Bazar can be chaotic, with many vendors calling out to you. Here is the strategy:

1.  **Observe the Locals:** Look for stalls where large numbers of Odia families or locals are buying. They know the correct prices and the best quality.
2.  **Ask for a "100 Rupee Thali":** Instead of ordering individual items by name (which can get confusing), you can approach a vendor and ask, *"Bhai, sahe tanka ra thali gote diantu"* (Brother, give me a ₹100 plate). They will serve a mix of rice, dal, and curries on a plantain leaf.
3.  **Eat in the Designated Areas:** There are designated seating areas within Ananda Bazar where you can sit cross-legged on the floor and eat. Ensure your mother finds a comfortable spot.
4.  **Avoid Middlemen:** Do not ask a random Panda roaming near the main gate to "arrange" Mahaprasad for you unless you trust him. They will add a heavy commission. Walk directly into Ananda Bazar and buy it straight from the earthen pots of the *Suaras*.
5.  **Wash Your Hands Properly:** Eat with your right hand. Washing facilities are available nearby. 

## The Taste of Devotion

The food is cooked entirely in earthen pots over wood fires, using only local, indigenous vegetables (no potatoes, tomatoes, or green chilies are used, as they are not native to India). The resulting flavor is earthy, smoky, and unlike anything you will eat in a modern restaurant.
