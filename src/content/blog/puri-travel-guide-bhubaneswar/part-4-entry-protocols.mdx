# Part 4: Temple Entry Protocols, The "Hindu" Rule, and Dress Code

Approaching the majestic *Singha Dwar* (Lion's Gate) of the Shree Jagannath Temple is a moment of awe. However, before you cross the threshold guarded by the statues of Jaya and Vijaya, you must adhere to some of the strictest temple protocols in India. The temple administration (SJTA) is uncompromising on these rules to preserve the sanctity and ancient traditions of the shrine.

## The "Only Orthodox Hindus Allowed" Rule

One of the most widely discussed rules of the Jagannath Temple is the restriction on entry: **Only orthodox Hindus are permitted inside.**

This rule has a deep historical and traditional basis, rooted in protecting the deity from historical invasions. Over the centuries, several prominent figures, including Lord Curzon, Indira Gandhi (due to her marriage to a non-Hindu), and B.R. Ambedkar, were famously denied entry. 

### How is "Hindu" Proven?

You asked: *"What is the base to prove that I am a Hindu? Should I carry an Aadhaar card?"*

There is no formal "test" or interrogation at the gate. The temple guards and priests rely on a combination of factors:
1.  **Appearance and Attire:** Wearing traditional Indian clothing, displaying common Hindu symbols (like a tilak or sacred thread, though not mandatory), and speaking an Indian language generally signal your identity.
2.  **Name:** In cases of doubt, authorities may ask for your name. 

**Should you carry your Aadhaar Card?** 
**Yes, absolutely.** While 99% of Indian citizens are never asked for ID at the gate, carrying your Aadhaar card is crucial. If any overzealous priest or guard questions your origin (which occasionally happens to Indians who look "different" or have a non-traditional appearance), your Aadhaar card proves your nationality and your name. Keep it safely tucked in a pocket.

*Note: For non-Hindus, the alternative is to view the temple from the roof of the adjacent Raghunandan Library.*

## The Strict Dress Code

In recent years, the temple administration has strictly enforced a dress code to maintain decorum. You cannot enter in casual, western wear. 

### For You (Men):
*   **Ideal:** Dhoti-Kurta or Pajama-Kurta. 
*   **Acceptable:** Formal trousers and a conservative shirt. 
*   **Strictly Banned:** Shorts, half-pants, ripped jeans, sleeveless shirts (banyans), and any clothing deemed disrespectful. 

### For Your Mother (Women):
*   **Ideal:** Saree or Salwar-Kameez with a dupatta. 
*   **Strictly Banned:** Skirts, shorts, ripped jeans, tight western clothing, or sleeveless tops. 

If you arrive in prohibited clothing, you will be turned away at the barricades. There are shops near the temple selling affordable dhotis and drapes if you need a quick change, but it is best to arrive fully prepared from Bhubaneswar.

## Footwear: The Burning Floors of May

You must remove your shoes before entering. 

*   **Shoe Stands:** There are free and nominal-fee (₹5 - ₹10) shoe stands maintained by the temple administration near the gates. You will be given a token. Do not lose this token!
*   **Slippers vs. Shoes:** You asked if you should carry slippers. **Yes.** Wear cheap, simple slippers that are easy to take off and not a huge loss if misplaced. Do not wear expensive sneakers. 
*   **The May Heat:** You are traveling on May 9. By 10:00 AM, the stone floors of the temple courtyard will be scalding hot. The temple administration usually lays down coir/jute carpets and occasionally sprays water to protect devotees' feet, but be prepared for a hot walk. 

With your attire checked and your shoes deposited, the next major hurdle is security. The Jagannath Temple has a zero-tolerance policy for electronics and leather. We will cover this "Digital Detox" extensively in Part 5.
